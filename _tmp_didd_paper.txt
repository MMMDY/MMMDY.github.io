Critique  ability, deﬁned as the capacity  to identify and rectify ﬂaws in text generation,  is crucial for the ap-
plications  of Large Language  Models (LLMs). As a meta-cognitive  capability,  enhancing  the critique ability of 
LLMs poses signiﬁcant  challenges.  Recent studies have proposed  improving  this ability through  ﬁne-tuning  on 
critique datasets.  However,  the static data distribution  of existing datasets  often leads to a mismatch  between  
the training  data and the diverse optimization  needs of target models, thereby hindering  their eﬀectiveness.  To 
address this issue, we introduce  a novel Dynamic  Iterative  Data Distribution  Optimization  Method (DIDD) that 
dynamically  adjusts training  data distributions  to align with the speciﬁc optimization  requirements  of target 
models. Speciﬁcally,  DIDD detects the vulnerable  data distribution  of target optimization  models by conducting  
the meta-critique  on synthesized  test set. The detected  vulnerable  data distribution  are then leveraged  to con-
struct the training  dataset that aligns with target model more closely, improving  the eﬀectiveness  of the training  
dataset. Extensive  experimental  results across four benchmarks  demonstrate  that our proposed  DIDD eﬀectively  
alleviates  the mismatch  between  the training  dataset and target optimization  models.
1.  Introduction
The critique  ability has been widely used in automatic  evaluation  
and self-improvement  of Large Language  Models  (LLMs) (Lan et al., 
2024b ; Wu et al., 2024 ), aiming to detect and revise ﬂaws in generated  
text. Recent works have demonstrated  that only advanced  and large-
scale LLMs exhibit reliable  critique  ability (Lan et al., 2024b ; Lin et al., 
2024a ), while their inference  eﬃciency  are usually  ineﬃcient.  There-
fore, numerous  works propose  to improve  the critique  ability of eﬃcient  
LLMs by ﬁne-tuning  on critique  datasets  (Kim et al., 2024a ; Lan et al., 
2024a ; Zheng et al., 2023b ). As a meta-cognition  ability of LLMs (Toy 
et al., 2024 ; Wang & Zhao , 2024 ), critique  ability is complex,  and it 
remains  challenging  to improve  it (Lan et al., 2024b ; Lin et al., 2024a ).
Early studies primarily  rely on human-annotated  critique  datasets  
to optimize  models,  such as Shepherd  (Wang et al., 2023b ) and Crit-

To address  this challenges,  this paper proposes  a novel Dynamic
Iterative  Data Distribution  Optimization  Method  (DIDD),  which dynam-
ically optimizing  and data distribution  tailored  to target optimization  
models.  Speciﬁcally,  DIDD dynamically  generates  high-quality  synthetic  
data and optimizes  the model through  the following  four steps: 1) Syn-
thesize test samples  based on the initial data distribution  and collect 
the inference  results of target model; 2) Conduct  meta-critique,  which 
assesses  target model’s  outputs  against  reference  test samples,  counts 
the error rates and detects the vulnerable  data distributions;  3) Mixture  
the initial data distribution  with vulnerable  data distributions,  which 
is used to construct  the training  dataset  tailored  for target model; 4) 
Optimize  the target model on the training  dataset  and proceed  to the 
next iteration  of data generation  and optimization.  The data synthesis  
and model optimization  processes  in DIDD are dynamic,  enabling  eﬀec-
tively tracking  of the model’s  vulnerable  data distributions  and targeted  
enhancement.
In this paper, we systematically  analyze  two crucial types of data 
distribution  in critique:  1) Data Type Distribution : Diverse  data types 
are crucial for improving  model generalization  capabilities  (Li et al., 
2024b ; Ouyang  et al., 2022b ); 2) Quality  Distribution  of Generated  
Text: Previous  works have demonstrated  that the quality of evaluated  
text inﬂuences  the critique  ability of LLMs (Lan et al., 2024b ). It is worth 
noting that while this study focuses  on these two data type distributions,  
the DIDD method  is ﬂexible  and can be extended  to any speciﬁc  data 
distribution  settings.
Extensive  experimental  results on four widely-used  critique  bench-
marks demonstrate  the superiority  of our proposed  DIDD method.  In-
ternLM2-7B-Chat  and Llama3-8B-Instruct  models  that ﬁne-tuned  by our 
DIDD methods  signiﬁcantly  outperforms  existing  7B-13B  baselines  on 
single-wise  and pair-wise  evaluation  protocols,  indicating  the better 
quality of our generated  datasets  than previous  works. Moreover,  ab-
lation studies prove the contributions  of detected  vulnerable  data dis-
tribution  of target optimization  model during ﬁne-tuning.  Finally,  our 
proposed  DIDD also exhibit better data eﬃciency  than previous  data 
synthesize  methods.  The contributions  of our work are three-fold:
•We conduct  the systematic  analysis  about the inﬂuence  of data dis-
tribution  on model’s  critique  ability.
•We propose  a novel DIDD method  to address  the issue of the mis-
match between  the critique  training  dataset  and target optimization  
model.
•Extensive  experimental  results demonstrate  the eﬀectiveness  of our 
proposed  DIDD method  on both single-wise  and pair-wise  critique  
tasks. Our 7B critique  models  ﬁne-tuned  by DIDD signiﬁcantly  out-
performs  existing  7B-13B  state-of-the-art  baselines.
2.  Related  work
Critique  ability of LLMs. Recently,  the critique  ability of LLMs has been 
widely applied  in two scenarios.
(1)Automatic  evaluation  of generated  text (Saunders  et al., 2022 ; Tian 
et al., 2024 ; Zheng et al., 2023b ): GPTScore  (Fu et al., 2023 ) and 
G-Eval (Liu et al., 2023 ) propose  to leverage  LLMs to evaluate  qual-
ity of generated  text by designing  the evaluation  prompt  and gen-
erating  the chain-of-thought  explanation,  which signiﬁcantly  out-
performs  the several automatic  evaluation  paradigm  (Wang et al., 
2023a ), such as heuristic  automatic  evaluation  (Banerjee  & Lavie , 
2005 ; Lin, 2004 ; Papineni  et al., 2001 ), embedding-based  automatic  
evaluation  (Zhang et al., 2020 ) and learning-based  automatic  evalu-
ation (Huang et al., 2020 ; Lan et al., 2020 ; Tao et al., 2017 ; Zhang 
et al., 2022 );(2)Self-improvement  of LLMs: the critique  ability is also widely used in 
improving  the LLM’s general  capability  without  any human super-
vision. For example,  Self-Rewarding  (Yuan et al., 2024 ) and Meta-
Rewarding  (Wu et al., 2024 ) synthesize  the preference  dataset  using 
LLM itself as the judge model, which are then used to ﬁne-tune  the 
LLMs with DPO method  (Rafailov  et al., 2023 ).
Improve  critique  ability. Numerous  works demonstrate  that critique  abil-
ity of LLMs also exhibit the scaling phenomena  (Kaplan et al., 2020 ), 
and advanced  and large-scale  LLMs exhibit much better critique  abil-
ity than small-scale  models.  However,  the expensive  inference  cost of 
large-scale  LLMs hinders  their application  in large-scale  evaluation  sce-
narios. Therefore,  numerous  works propose  to improve  the critique  abil-
ity of eﬃcient  LLMs by ﬁne-tuning  them on the constructed  critique  
dataset.  For example,  Auto-J (Li et al., 2024b ), UltraCM  (Cui et al., 
2023 ) and Prometheus  (Kim et al., 2024a ) optimize  the small-scale  
open-source  LLMs, like Llama series models,  by conducting  the super-
vised ﬁne-tuning  on synthesized  dataset  generated  by GPT-4. Moreover,  
Themis  (Hu et al., 2024 ), SFR-Judge  (Rafailov  et al., 2024 ), MultiCri-
tique (Lan et al., 2024a ) Critic-RM  (Yu et al., 2024 ) leverages  the pref-
erence learning  methods,  like DPO and Reinforcement  Learning  (RL), 
to optimize  the critique  ability by distinguishing  the good and bad cri-
tiques.
Training  Data Source. Based on the source of the training  dataset,  exist-
ing works could be classiﬁed  into three categories:
(1)Human-annotated  Dataset:  This method  has demonstrated  eﬀective-
ness in improving  critique  ability, as exempliﬁed  by Shepherd  (Wang 
et al., 2023b ) and CriticGPT  (McAleese  et al., 2024 ). While human-
annotated  datasets  are eﬀective,  their expensive  annotation  cost hin-
ders their scalability,  especially  for annotating  the long critique  text.
(2)Synthetic  Dataset:  This method  improves  the critique  capability  of 
LLMs by mimicking  advanced  LLM’s behavior,  like GPT-4 (Li et al., 
2024b ). Although  this kinds of methods  could easily scale up, the 
generated  training  samples  might contains  the noise, inﬂuencing  
their eﬀectiveness  (Verga et al., 2024 ).
(3)Human-Model  collaborative  Dataset:  recently,  numerous  works con-
struct the critique  dataset  by validating  the model-generated  samples  
with human-annotated  labels, SFR-Judge  (Wang et al., 2024 ), Critic-
RM (Yu et al., 2024 ) and SCRIT (Tang et al., 2025 ). This method  
achieves  a balance  between  data construction  quality and the hu-
man annotation  cost. However,  they are still limited  by the human 
annotation,  making  them hard to scale up.
The data distributions  of these three kinds of datasets  are static, lead-
ing to a mismatch  with the target optimization  model. This paper pro-
pose to address  this problem  by dynamically  adjusting  the data distri-
bution during model optimization.  Since Human-annotated  Dataset  and 
Human-model  collaborative  Dataset  are not ﬂexible  and hard to scale 
up, we only conduct  experiments  on the Synthetic  Dataset.

selectively  querying  the most informative  samples  for labeling  (Settles , 
2009 ). For example,  recent advances  in deep active learning  (DAL) in-
tegrate neural networks  with sophisticated  sampling  strategies,  such as 
uncertainty,  diversity,  and hybrid approaches,  signiﬁcantly  improving  
performance  in image classiﬁcation  and natural  language  processing  (Li 
et al., 2024a ). More recently,  generative  AI has begun to reshape  AL 
frameworks  by synthesizing  high-quality  training  instances  or simulat-
ing diverse  scenarios,  reducing  reliance  on real labeled  data (ElSayary , 
2024 ). Hybrid approaches  combining  LLM-based  reasoning  with tradi-
tional AL criteria–such  as entropy  or margin  sampling–are  emerging  as 
promising  directions  to improve  query quality and model generaliza-
tion (Rouzegar  & Makrehchi , 2024 ). These integrations  not only lower Expert  Systems  With Applications  300 (2026)  129878

While both DIDD and Active Learning  (AL) aim to optimize  model 
performance  through  data selection,  DIDD diverges  fundamentally  in 
two aspects:  (1) AL selects individual  instances  (e.g., ambiguous  sam-
ples) to improve  model decision  boundaries.  DIDD operates  at the distri-
bution granularity:  it identiﬁes  systematic  weaknesses  in data coverage  
and dynamically  synthesizes  data to ﬁll gaps. This corrects  underlying  
data biases rather than patching  localized  errors; (2) AL iterates  between  
static data pools and model updates.  While DIDD creates a dynamic  pro-
cess: each iteration’s  model optimization  reﬁnes the subsequent  data 
synthesis,  progressively  aligning  distributions  with the model’s  evolving  
capabilities.  This ensures  scalability  to complex  critique  tasks where AL 
struggles  with distribution  shifts. 
3.  Preliminary  study
In this section,  we conduct  the preliminary  study to analyze  the dif-
ference  among data distribution,  proving  the importance  of the data 
distribution  for improving  critique  ability of LLMs.
3.1.  Task formulation
So far, there are two kinds of critique  protocol:  single-wise  and pair-
wise critiques.
Single-Wise  Critique.  aims to directly  critique  the quality of one gener-
ated response  (𝑅) for a given the query 𝑄 based on the chosen evaluation  
criteria  cri.: (𝑟, 𝑆) =CriticModel (𝑄, 𝑅, cri.), where 𝑟 is the textual ratio-
nale for analyzing  the quality of 𝑅 (Zheng et al., 2023a ), and 𝑆 is the quality score reﬂecting  the overall quality of 𝑅 (Zheng et al., 2023b ). 
The higher score indicates  better quality.
Pair-wise  Critique.  is another  widely-used  critique  protocol  (Li 
et al., 2024b ), aiming to distinguish  two generations  (𝑅𝐴, 𝑅𝐵) 
and choose the better one given the evaluation  criteria  cri.: 
(𝑟, 𝐿) =CriticModel (𝑄,cri., 𝑅𝐴, 𝑅𝐵), where 𝐿 is the preference  
label that indicates  which generation  is better. Compared  with single-
wise critique  protocol,  the pair-wise  critique  protocol  is more robust 
and objective  (Li et al., 2024c ).
3.2.  Analyze  the inﬂuence  of data distribution
This subsection  aims to analyze  how two data distribution  in cri-
tique training  data: data type distribution  and the quality distribution  
of generated  texts, aﬀects the improvements  of LLM’s critique  ability, 
and summarize  the high-quality  heuristic  data distribution,  which pro-
vide supports  for our proposed  DIDD method.
3.2.1.  Data distribution
This study focuses  on analyzing  two crucial data distributions  for 
single-wise  and pair-wise  critiques  in critique  datasets:  data type distri-
bution and the quality distribution  of generated  text.
Data type distribution.  As shown in Table 1, this section primarily  ana-
lyzes the eight common  data types (Li et al., 2024b ).
Quality distribution  of generated  texts. As shown in Table 2, in the single-
wise critique  protocol,  the generated  text quality can be classiﬁed  into 
low-quality,  medium-quality  and high-quality.  In the pair-wise  critique  
protocol,  the quality can be categorized  into three kinds of diﬃculties:  
simple-diﬃculty,  moderate-diﬃculty  and hard-diﬃculty.  This classiﬁ-
cation provides  a more nuanced  understanding  of the critique  task and 
helps evaluate  the model’s  performance  under varying  levels of text 
quality and complexity.

3.4.  Analysis  on the quality distribution  of generated  texts
This section analyzes  the quality distribution  of generated  texts un-
der single-wise  and pair-wise  critique  protocols.
(1) Single-wise  critique  results. As shown in Fig. 2, compared  to low-
quality and high-quality  generated  texts, medium-quality  generated  
texts contributed  more signiﬁcantly  to improving  the model’s  critique  
ability. For instance,  in terms of the quality of generated  critiques  (mid-
dle panel of Fig. 2), models  trained  on medium-quality  generated  text 
data achieved  signiﬁcantly  higher performance  on the dev and test sets 
compared  to low-quality  and high-quality  text. This phenomenon  sug-
gests that the medium-quality  generated  text exhibits  more diverse  er-
ror types and patterns.  Therefore,  the critiques  for the medium-quality  
samples  contain  more dense training  signals,  which is beneﬁcial  to the 
model optimization.  Further  analysis  of the impact of the proportion  of 
medium-quality  generated  text data on model performance  (Fig. 3(a)) 
reveals that as the proportion  of medium-quality  generated  text in-
creases,  the model’s  critique  ability gradually  improves,  reaching  its 
peak when the proportion  is between  0.6 and 0.8. However,  when the 
training  data consists  entirely  of medium-quality  generated  text, the 
model’s  performance  drops signiﬁcantly.  This indicates  that the qual-
ity distribution  of the generated  text to be evaluated  must remain di-
verse. Moreover,  this study also examines  the impact of the proportion

5.  Experimental  results  and analysis
This section provides  a comprehensive  evaluation  of the proposed  
DIDD method  on single-wise  and pair-wise  evaluation  protocols.  We aim to assess not only the absolute  performance  improvements  brought  by 
DIDD, but also its generalization  ability across diﬀerent  model architec-
tures and benchmark  datasets.
5.1.  Baselines  and experimental  setup
5.1.1.  Comparison  models and datasets
Single-wise  critique  evaluation.  To verify the eﬀectiveness  of the DIDD 
method,  we compare  it with eight state-of-the-art  closed-source  and 
open-source  models,  including  GPT-3.5-turbo,  GPT-4-turbo,  Qwen2-7B-
Instruct  (Team , 2024 ), Themis-8B  (Hu et al., 2024 ), TIGERScore-7B,  
TIGER Score-13B  (Jiang et al., 2024 ), UltraCM-13B  (Cui et al., 2023 ), 
CritiqueLLM-6B  (Ke et al., 2024 ) and Auto-J-13B  (Li et al., 2024b ). 
Additionally,  we also compare  the widely-used  UltraFeedback  critique  
dataset  (Cui et al., 2023 ).
Pair-wise  critique  evaluation.  Our proposed  DIDD is compared  with four 
advanced  models,  including  GPT-3.5-turbo,  GPT-4-turbo,  Qwen2-7B-
Instruct  (Team , 2024 ), and Auto-J-13B  (Li et al., 2024b ). Moreover,  we 
also compare  the widely-used  Auto-J pair-wise  critique  dataset.
Moreover,  the baseline  that trained  with our proposed  heuristic  data 
distribution  𝐷Heuristic is also evaluated,  and the data scales of both meth-
ods are kept the same to ensure fairness.
5.1.2.  Experimental  setup
All experiments  utilize GPT-4o  to synthesize  training  data, which are 
used to train several base models:  InternLM2-7B-Chat  (Cai et al., 2024 ), 
Llama-3-8B-Instruct  and Qwen2.5-0.5B/1.5B/3B/7B  models  (Team , 
2024 ). The initial base model 𝑀0 for the ﬁrst iteration  is initialized  
from InternLM2-7B-Chat  and Llama-3-8B-Instruct,  respectively.  The de-
fault test data size of our proposed  DIDD method  is set as 400. More 
details of model training  and hyperparameter  settings  can be found in 
Section  3.2.2 .
5.1.3.  Evaluation  benchmarks
The evaluation  benchmarks  for the single-wise  critique  task is the 
same as the setup described  in Section  3.2.2 . In the pair-wise  critique  
tasks, in addition  to CriticEval  benchmark,  we also supplement  the 
Auto-J (Li et al., 2024b ) and PreferenceBench  (Kim et al., 2024b ) bench-
marks. Auto-J and PreferenceBench  use accuracy  (Acc.) as the objective  
evaluation  metric to measure  the consistency  between  model judgments  
and human-annotated  preference.  Since Auto-J,  PreferenceBench  and
CriticBench  don’t include  the dev set, we only report the model’s  per-
formance  on CriticEval  dev set. Besides,  this dev set is not used for 
any training  procedure,  like early stopping.  Both dev and test sets were 
used strictly for performance  evaluation.

5.5.  Distribution  mixture  hyper-parameter  analysis
DIDD generates  a mixed data distribution  𝐷mix by combining  the 
model’s  vulnerable  data distribution  𝐷𝑀𝑡 with the heuristic  data dis-
tribution  𝐷Heuristic using a hyper-parameter  𝛼. The mixing ratio 𝛼 is a 
critical hyperparameter  aﬀecting  model performance.  This section in-
vestigates  the impact of 𝐷𝑀𝑡 on model performance  by adjusting  𝛼 to 
control  the proportion  of 𝐷𝑀𝑡 in 𝐷mix.
As shown in Fig. 9(a) and (b), increasing  the proportion  of 𝐷𝑀𝑡 leads 
to a signiﬁcant  trend where model performance  ﬁrst improves  and then 
declines  in both single-wise  and pair-wise  critique  tasks. For single-wise  
critique  tasks, the model achieves  optimal  performance  when 𝐷𝑀𝑡=
0.5or0.6, while for pair-wise  critique  tasks, the optimal  performance  is 
observed  at 𝐷𝑀𝑡= 0.4. Moreover,  the experimental  results indicate  that 
the performance  of the mixed data distribution  𝐷mix consistently  sur-
passes that of both the pure heuristic  data distribution  (𝐷𝑀𝑡= 0) and 
the pure model vulnerable  data distribution  (𝐷𝑀𝑡= 1.0). This demon-
strates that both the heuristic  data distribution  and the model’s  vul-
nerable  data distribution  play crucial roles in optimizing  model perfor-
mance.  By combining  the two distributions,  the quality of the data can

6.  Conclusions  and future works
In this paper, we propose  a novel Dynamic  Iterative  Data Distribu-
tion Optimization  method  (DIDD)  tailored  to target optimization  mod-
els, addressing  the mismatch  between  the training  dataset  distribution  
and target optimization  model. The DIDD method  employs  a “Detection-
then-Optimization”  mechanism  for joint data distribution  and model op-
timization.  Extensive  experimental  results on four benchmarks  demon-
strate that, DIDD eﬀectively  mitigates  the mismatch  between  training  
data distributions  and target optimization  models,  signiﬁcantly  enhanc-
ing the model’s  critique  ability compared  to existing  strong baselines.  In 
the future, we will explore  how to extend DIDD to more complex  gen-
eration  tasks, such as multimodal  critiques,  code generation  critiques.
7.  Limitations
In this section,  we discuss two limitations  of our proposed  DIDD 
method:  (1) Eﬀectiveness  of DIDD depends  on meta-critique  reli-
ability:  Although  we enhanced  meta-critique  by synthesizing  reference  
critiques  (Section  4.2) (Lan et al., 2024a ,b; Sun et al., 2024 ), our experi-
ments show that meta-critique  remains  a bottleneck,  particularly  due to 
noise introduced  by test data scaling.  For example,  increasing  test data scale initially  improves  performance  but eventually  degrades  it due to 
ampliﬁed  noise, as shown in Fig. 10(a) and (b). This highlights  the need 
for more robust meta-critique  methods  in future work; (2) Heuristic  
data distribution  is limited:  DIDD focuses  on data type and quality 
distributions  (Section  3.2), but real-world  scenarios  involve  more di-
verse and dynamic  distributions.  In this paper, we observe  that assign-
ing higher sample rate for high-contribution  data type (like medium-
quality generated  text, Fig. 3) leads to better performance.  However,  it is 
challenging  to analyze  the complex  data distribution.  We acknowledge  
that manually  deﬁning  these heuristic  data distributions  is a preliminary  
step, which may limit DIDD’s ﬂexibility.  Future work will explore  auto-
mated methods  to identify  complex  distributions  of downstream  tasks, 
broadening  DIDD’s applicability.
